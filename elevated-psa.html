Comprehensive PSA Evaluation — Understanding Your Results, What Comes Next, and When a Biopsy Is Actually Needed

Your PSA Is Elevated. What Does That Actually Mean?

An elevated PSA does not mean you have prostate cancer. But it does mean something needs to be looked at — carefully, methodically, and without panic.

PSA (Prostate-Specific Antigen) is a protein produced by the prostate gland. It is present in the blood of all men with a prostate, and some elevation is normal. What matters is why the PSA is elevated — because the causes range from a benign enlarged prostate to prostatitis to, in some cases, cancer.

Dr. Kashani's approach is thorough but measured. He uses a full suite of clinical tools — repeat PSA, free PSA ratio, PSA density, digital rectal exam, and when appropriate, multiparametric MRI — to determine whether a biopsy is warranted before recommending one. The goal is accuracy, not reflexive testing.

Do not ignore an elevated PSA: While most elevated PSA results are not due to cancer, the cases where cancer is found are the cases where early detection saves lives. A proper evaluation is always the right call.

What Is PSA and What Causes It to Rise?

PSA is produced by prostate cells — both normal and abnormal ones. A small amount is always present in the blood. When the prostate is enlarged, inflamed, irritated, or cancerous, more PSA leaks into the bloodstream and the measured level rises.

The most common causes of elevated PSA — in order of frequency — are:

Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia (BPH)

An enlarged prostate produces more PSA simply because there is more prostate tissue. This is the most common benign cause. A large prostate with a PSA of 6 may be perfectly explained by its size alone.

Prostatitis

Inflammation or infection of the prostate can dramatically elevate PSA — sometimes into ranges that look alarming. After treating prostatitis, PSA often normalizes. Repeat testing after treatment is always part of the evaluation.

Recent Procedures or Manipulation

Prostate biopsy, catheterization, cystoscopy, or even vigorous bicycle riding can temporarily elevate PSA. Ejaculation within 48 hours of testing can also raise results. Timing of the test matters.

Prostate Cancer

Cancer cells produce PSA in an abnormal, less-regulated way. Prostate cancer is an important cause of elevated PSA — but it accounts for a minority of cases. Not every elevated PSA represents cancer, but every elevated PSA deserves an evaluation.

Age-Related PSA Rise

PSA naturally increases with age even in men with healthy prostates. This is why age-specific PSA thresholds are more useful than a single cutoff. A PSA of 3.0 in a 50-year-old warrants closer attention than the same value in a 75-year-old.

Medications That Lower PSA

5-alpha reductase inhibitors (finasteride, dutasteride) used to treat BPH reduce PSA by approximately 50% after 6 months. If you take these medications, your PSA must be doubled before interpretation. Failure to account for this leads to missed cancers.

The PSA Evaluation Process

A systematic, stepwise approach — not reflexive biopsy

How Dr. Kashani Evaluates an Elevated PSA

An elevated PSA does not lead directly to a biopsy. Before recommending any invasive procedure, Dr. Kashani works through a systematic evaluation designed to separate men who need a biopsy from those who do not.

Step 1

Clinical History & Symptom Review

Dr. Kashani reviews your PSA history (trend over time is often more informative than a single value), urinary symptoms, medications, family history of prostate cancer, and any recent events that could have transiently elevated your PSA.

PSA velocity — the rate of change over time — is a powerful tool. A rapidly rising PSA is more concerning than a stable one, regardless of the absolute number.

Step 2

Digital Rectal Exam (DRE)

A physical examination of the prostate through the rectum allows Dr. Kashani to assess prostate size, symmetry, and texture. A smooth, symmetrical prostate suggests BPH. A nodule, firmness, or asymmetry raises concern for cancer regardless of PSA level.

DRE and PSA together are more informative than either test alone. A normal PSA does not rule out cancer, and an abnormal DRE with a normal PSA still warrants further evaluation.

Key Test

Free PSA Ratio

PSA circulates in two forms: bound (attached to proteins) and free. Cancer cells produce PSA that is predominantly bound. A low free PSA ratio (under 10–15%) suggests higher cancer probability. A high free PSA ratio suggests BPH or other benign causes. This test is most useful when total PSA is in the 4–10 ng/mL range.

Free PSA ratio is one of Dr. Kashani's most used tools in the gray-zone PSA range — it can spare a patient from biopsy or, conversely, identify a case that needs urgent attention.

Step 3

PSA Density

PSA density corrects for prostate size by dividing total PSA by prostate volume. A large prostate naturally produces more PSA. A PSA density above 0.15 ng/mL/cc suggests the PSA elevation may not be fully explained by prostate size alone — and warrants closer attention.

Prostate volume is measured by ultrasound or MRI. This calculation takes one minute but can meaningfully change the risk assessment.

Step 4

Multiparametric MRI of the Prostate

When the clinical picture warrants further evaluation — but before committing to a biopsy — a multiparametric MRI of the prostate provides detailed imaging of the prostate's internal anatomy. Suspicious areas are scored using the PI-RADS system (1 to 5). PI-RADS 1–2 lesions are very unlikely to be significant cancer. PI-RADS 4–5 lesions are concerning and guide targeted biopsy.

MRI before biopsy has become the standard of care for elevated PSA evaluation. It detects more clinically significant cancers while reducing unnecessary biopsy in men with low-risk findings.

Step 5 (When Indicated)

MRI-Targeted Prostate Biopsy

When biopsy is recommended, Dr. Kashani performs or coordinates MRI-fusion targeted biopsy — where the MRI findings are fused with real-time ultrasound to direct biopsy needles precisely to suspicious areas. This is more accurate than traditional systematic biopsy and minimizes the number of cores taken.

Not every elevated PSA leads to a biopsy. When biopsy is necessary, targeted biopsy is the right approach — more accurate, fewer unnecessary samples, and lower infection risk.

Frequently Asked Questions — Elevated PSA

What PSA level is considered elevated?

Traditionally, a PSA above 4.0 ng/mL was considered elevated — but this is an oversimplification. PSA must be interpreted in context: a PSA of 3.5 in a 45-year-old man is more concerning than a PSA of 5.0 in a 75-year-old man with a large, benign prostate. Dr. Kashani evaluates PSA alongside age, prostate volume, PSA velocity, and free PSA ratio to determine the true level of concern and the most appropriate next step.

Does an elevated PSA always mean prostate cancer?

No — an elevated PSA is not a cancer diagnosis. The most common causes of elevated PSA are benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH), prostatitis, and ejaculation within 48 hours of the test. Prostate cancer is one possible cause but not the most common. That said, elevated PSA always warrants evaluation — because when cancer is present, early detection significantly improves outcomes.

What is the free PSA ratio and why does it matter?

PSA circulates in the blood in two forms: bound (attached to proteins) and free. The free PSA ratio — the percentage of PSA that is unbound — helps distinguish benign from malignant causes. A lower free PSA ratio (under 10–15%) suggests a higher probability of prostate cancer. A higher free PSA ratio suggests a benign cause such as BPH. This test is particularly helpful when total PSA is in the 4–10 ng/mL range.

Do I need a prostate biopsy if my PSA is elevated?

Not necessarily — and Dr. Kashani does not reflexively recommend biopsy for every elevated PSA. The decision depends on the full clinical picture: total PSA level, free PSA ratio, PSA velocity, digital rectal exam findings, prostate volume, and ideally a multiparametric MRI result. MRI-targeted biopsy dramatically improves biopsy accuracy while reducing the number of cores needed. A thoughtful evaluation always comes before any biopsy recommendation.

Can prostatitis cause an elevated PSA?

Yes — prostatitis is a common cause of significant PSA elevation, sometimes pushing PSA into ranges that look alarming on paper. If Dr. Kashani suspects prostatitis, he may treat it with antibiotics and repeat the PSA 4 to 6 weeks later. A PSA that returns to normal after treatment is highly reassuring and avoids unnecessary biopsy.

What is an MRI-targeted prostate biopsy?

A multiparametric MRI of the prostate before biopsy identifies suspicious areas within the prostate. An MRI-targeted (fusion) biopsy then directs biopsy needles precisely to those suspicious regions, rather than sampling the prostate randomly. This approach detects more clinically significant cancers and misses fewer — with fewer total needles required.

What does PSA density mean?

PSA density is the total PSA divided by the prostate volume measured by ultrasound or MRI. Because a larger prostate naturally produces more PSA, PSA density corrects for prostate size. A PSA density above 0.15 ng/mL/cc is generally considered more concerning for cancer than a lower density. A large prostate with a moderately elevated PSA may have a low density — reassuring. A small prostate with the same PSA would have a high density — warranting closer attention.
