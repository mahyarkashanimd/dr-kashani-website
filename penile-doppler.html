Penile Doppler Ultrasound on Long Island

Precision In-Office Vascular Testing — Identify the Exact Cause of Erectile Dysfunction

What Is Penile Doppler Ultrasound?

A penile Doppler ultrasound — also called a penile duplex ultrasound — is a specialized in-office imaging test that measures blood flow inside the penis. It identifies the underlying vascular cause of erectile dysfunction so that the most effective treatment can be selected.

Erections depend on precisely coordinated blood flow: arteries must deliver enough blood to fill the erectile chambers, and the veins must trap that blood inside. When either of these mechanisms fails, ED results. A penile Doppler ultrasound tells us exactly which part of that system is not working — and by how much.

The test uses a standard ultrasound probe combined with Doppler technology to measure the speed and direction of blood flow in the penile arteries and veins. Because the penis must be erect for accurate measurements, a small injection of medication is given first to produce an erection. The ultrasound measurements are then taken while the erection develops.

Not available everywhere: Penile duplex ultrasound requires specialized training and equipment. Dr. Kashani performs this test routinely in-office at both of his Long Island locations — it is not a service offered at most general urology practices.

Who Benefits from Penile Doppler Ultrasound?

This test is most useful when the cause of erectile dysfunction is unclear or when you have not responded to pills and injections as expected. It is part of a thorough evaluation rather than a first-line test for every man with ED.

ED Not Responding to Pills

If Viagra, Cialis, or Levitra have not worked well for you, a penile Doppler can reveal why — and whether injections, lifestyle changes, or a penile implant are likely to do better.

Peyronie's Disease Evaluation

Ultrasound can locate scar tissue (plaques) and calcifications in the penis, measure the degree of curvature, and assess blood flow — all critical for deciding between Xiaflex, traction therapy, or surgery.

Post-Surgical or Post-Radiation ED

Men with ED following prostate cancer surgery, pelvic radiation, or other pelvic procedures often have vascular changes that the Doppler can quantify and guide treatment for.

Diabetes or Cardiovascular Disease

These conditions directly damage the blood vessels supplying the penis. A penile Doppler maps that damage precisely and helps select the treatment most likely to overcome it.

Younger Men with Unexplained ED

ED in men under 45 is sometimes dismissed as psychological. A penile Doppler can confirm whether there is a real vascular issue — and in young men with arterial insufficiency, it may prompt evaluation for underlying cardiovascular risk.

Pre-Surgical Planning for Penile Implant

Before penile implant surgery, a duplex ultrasound provides a detailed picture of the vascular anatomy, detects any scar tissue or fibrosis, and confirms the implant is the right next step.

How the Test Works

The test is straightforward and performed entirely in the office. Most men find it much less daunting than they expected. Here is what happens, step by step.

1
Pre-Procedure

Review & Preparation

Dr. Kashani reviews your medical history, current medications, and ED symptoms. You are asked to avoid PDE5 inhibitors (Viagra, Cialis) for 24–48 hours before the appointment. No other preparation is needed.

5–10 minutes
2
Pharmacostimulation

Injection to Produce an Erection

A small amount of vasoactive medication (typically alprostadil, or a Trimix combination) is injected into the side of the penis using a very fine needle. This produces an erection within 5–10 minutes, independent of sexual arousal or nerve function — allowing objective measurement of the vascular system.

5–10 minutes to onset
3
Doppler Imaging

Ultrasound Measurement of Blood Flow

Once the erection develops, Dr. Kashani applies the Doppler probe to the base and along the shaft of the penis. He measures peak systolic velocity (PSV) — the speed of blood flowing into the penis during each heartbeat — and end-diastolic velocity (EDV) — the speed of blood flow at rest between beats. Multiple measurements are taken as the erection progresses.

15–25 minutes
4
Results & Discussion

Review Findings and Next Steps

Dr. Kashani reviews the velocity measurements with you and explains what they mean. Results are discussed at the same visit. A formal follow-up is scheduled to present a complete treatment plan based on your results, history, and goals.

Same visit

About the injection: The medication used is the same as penile injection therapy for ED — a well-established treatment that is very safe. The erection typically resolves on its own within 30 to 60 minutes. Dr. Kashani has reversal medication available in the office if needed.

What Penile Doppler Results Can Tell Us

The blood flow measurements produce one of three main findings. Each one points toward a different treatment path and explains why certain treatments work for some men but not others.

Low Blood Inflow

Arterial Insufficiency

A low peak systolic velocity (PSV below 25 cm/s) means the arteries supplying the penis are not delivering enough blood. This is the most common vascular finding and is closely linked to cardiovascular disease, diabetes, smoking, and high blood pressure.

Treatment direction: Oral medications may help; penile injections are often more reliable; a penile implant is the most durable long-term option.

Venous Leak

Corporal Veno-Occlusive Dysfunction

An elevated end-diastolic velocity (EDV above 5 cm/s) means blood is draining out of the penis too quickly for the chambers to stay filled. This is called venous leak and is one reason oral medications rarely provide satisfying results.

Treatment direction: Pills are typically ineffective; injections often work well; a penile implant provides the most reliable erections.

Normal Vascular Function

Neurogenic or Psychological ED

When both PSV and EDV are normal, the vascular system is working properly. The cause of ED is likely neurogenic (nerve-related) or psychological (anxiety, performance pressure, relationship factors). This is common in younger men.

Treatment direction: Oral medications usually work very well; behavioral approaches and counseling may also help.

Peyronie's disease findings: In men with Peyronie's disease, the ultrasound also identifies the location and density of scar tissue (plaques), detects calcifications, and assesses penile curvature. This information directly shapes the choice between Xiaflex injections, traction therapy, plication surgery, and penile implant with curvature correction.

Frequently Asked Questions About Penile Doppler Ultrasound

These are the questions men most commonly ask before and after the test.

Is penile Doppler ultrasound painful?

The test is well tolerated by most men. The injection uses a very fine needle and is similar to a standard penile injection — most men find it much less uncomfortable than they expected. The ultrasound itself involves gentle pressure with the probe but is not painful. Any discomfort during the erection phase is typically mild and brief.

What happens during the procedure?

Dr. Kashani will first review your history, then inject a small amount of medication into the side of the penis to cause an erection. Once you have an erection, he applies the ultrasound probe to measure blood flow velocities in the penile arteries. The measurements are taken over about 20 minutes as the erection develops. Results are reviewed with you at the same visit.

What happens to the erection after the test?

The erection from the injection typically lasts 30 to 60 minutes and resolves on its own. Rarely, an erection can last longer — if yours persists beyond 4 hours, contact the office or go to an emergency room. Dr. Kashani provides clear instructions before and after the test, and reversal medication is available in the office if needed.

What do the results actually show?

The test measures the speed of blood flowing in and out of the penis. A low peak systolic velocity (PSV) indicates arterial insufficiency — the arteries are not delivering enough blood. An elevated end-diastolic velocity (EDV) indicates venous leak — blood is draining out too quickly. Normal readings in both suggest the cause of ED is neurogenic or psychological. For Peyronie's disease, the ultrasound also locates scar tissue and calcifications.

How does this test change my treatment plan?

Results directly shape which treatment Dr. Kashani recommends. Men with arterial insufficiency may not respond well to pills alone and often benefit from injections or a penile implant. Men with venous leak rarely see satisfying results from oral medications — injections or a penile implant are usually more effective. Men with normal vascular function often do well with pills or behavioral therapy. Without this test, treatment selection involves more uncertainty.

Can the test also evaluate Peyronie's disease?

Yes — and it is routinely performed for this purpose. During the erect phase, the ultrasound can identify the location, size, and density of Peyronie's plaques, detect calcifications within the scar tissue, and assess the degree of curvature. This information is important for choosing between Xiaflex injections, traction therapy, plication surgery, or penile implant with curvature correction.

Is it covered by insurance?

In most cases, yes — when ordered for an appropriate medical indication such as erectile dysfunction evaluation, it is typically covered by insurance including Medicare. Dr. Kashani’s team will verify your specific coverage before scheduling and discuss any out-of-pocket costs in advance.

How long does the appointment take?

Plan for approximately 60 to 90 minutes total. This includes a brief pre-procedure review, the injection and erection phase (about 20 to 30 minutes of active imaging), and time to discuss your results with Dr. Kashani before you leave.

Do I need to prepare for the test?

Preparation is minimal. You should avoid PDE5 inhibitors (Viagra, Cialis, Levitra, Stendra) for 24 to 48 hours before the test, as they can affect the results. Wear comfortable, loose-fitting clothing. You may eat and drink normally. Dr. Kashani’s team will provide a complete set of instructions when you schedule.

Should I have this test before considering a penile implant?

Dr. Kashani generally includes penile Doppler in the evaluation for men who are considering a penile implant. Understanding the vascular picture helps with surgical planning and confirms the implant is the right next step. It is also useful earlier in the process — for men who have not responded to pills, for younger men with unexplained ED, or whenever the underlying cause is unclear.
