BPH & Urinary Health

Enlarged Prostate, Overactive Bladder & Urinary Problems — Causes, Diagnosis & All Treatment Options

Are Urinary Problems Affecting Your Life?

Waking up multiple times a night to urinate. A weak stream that takes forever. A sudden, urgent need to go that you can barely control. These are not just inconveniences — they are medical symptoms that deserve proper evaluation and treatment.

Urinary problems in men are extremely common — especially with age — and they are almost always treatable. The key is figuring out exactly what is causing your symptoms, because the right treatment depends entirely on the right diagnosis.

Dr. Kashani evaluates and treats the full spectrum of male urinary conditions — from an enlarged prostate (BPH) and overactive bladder (OAB), to more serious causes like elevated PSA and hematuria. His goal is to give you your quality of life back.

Don't assume it's just aging: Many men accept urinary symptoms as an inevitable part of getting older. They are not. Effective treatments exist for every severity level — from simple daily medication to minimally invasive procedures that take less than an hour.

Two Common Conditions — Different Causes, Different Treatments

Many men have symptoms of both BPH and overactive bladder simultaneously — and treating one without addressing the other leads to incomplete relief. Dr. Kashani evaluates both carefully before recommending treatment.

BPH — Enlarged Prostate

Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia (BPH) is a non-cancerous enlargement of the prostate gland that squeezes the urethra and restricts urine flow. It is the most common cause of urinary symptoms in men over 50. Symptoms are caused by the mechanical obstruction of the prostate pressing on the urinary channel.

OAB — Overactive Bladder

Overactive bladder is a condition where the bladder muscle contracts involuntarily — creating sudden, urgent needs to urinate that are difficult to control, often accompanied by frequent urination and sometimes leakage. OAB can exist on its own or alongside BPH, and requires different medications and treatments.

Symptoms — Which Do You Recognize?

These symptoms overlap but have distinct patterns. Identifying which predominate helps guide the right treatment.

Nocturia

Waking up 2 or more times per night to urinate. One of the most disruptive and common urinary symptoms — affecting sleep quality, energy, and daily function.

Weak or Slow Stream

Urine flow that is weaker than it used to be, takes longer to start, or stops and starts. A classic sign of obstruction from an enlarged prostate.

Urgency

A sudden, strong urge to urinate that is difficult or impossible to delay. Can lead to rushing to the bathroom and, in some cases, leakage before reaching the toilet.

Frequency

Urinating more than 8 times per day. Can be caused by BPH, OAB, high fluid intake, or other conditions including UTI and diabetes.

Incomplete Emptying

The feeling that the bladder never fully empties — often leading to returning to the bathroom shortly after urinating. A sign of significant obstruction or poor bladder contractility.

Post-Void Dribbling

Continued dripping of urine after finishing urination. Caused by urine pooling in the urethra, common in BPH. Frustrating and often socially embarrassing.

How Are These Conditions Diagnosed?

Dr. Kashani uses a thorough evaluation to understand the severity of your symptoms and identify the underlying cause before recommending any treatment.

  • Detailed symptom history using the validated IPSS questionnaire
  • Digital rectal exam to assess prostate size and texture
  • PSA blood test to screen for prostate cancer
  • Urinalysis to rule out infection or blood in the urine
  • Urine flow rate (uroflowmetry) to measure stream strength
  • Post-void residual — ultrasound to measure retained urine
  • Cystoscopy when indicated — direct visualization of the urethra and bladder

Important: BPH symptoms and prostate cancer can overlap. Before treating BPH, Dr. Kashani always ensures an appropriate PSA evaluation has been completed and documented.

BPH & Enlarged Prostate — Treatment Options

From watchful waiting and daily medication to minimally invasive procedures and surgery

Medical Management for BPH

For mild to moderate BPH symptoms, medication is usually the first step. Several classes of drugs are available — and importantly, one of them (tadalafil) also treats erectile dysfunction at the same time.

First Line

Alpha Blockers

Alpha blockers relax the smooth muscle in the prostate and bladder neck, improving urine flow almost immediately. They do not shrink the prostate but significantly reduce obstruction symptoms. Results are often noticeable within days.

Tamsulosin (Flomax®) Silodosin (Rapaflo®) Alfuzosin Doxazosin

Most commonly prescribed first. Flomax (tamsulosin) is the most widely used and well-tolerated option.

Shrinks the Prostate

5-Alpha Reductase Inhibitors

These medications work by blocking the hormone that drives prostate growth — actually shrinking the prostate over 3 to 6 months. They are most effective in men with larger prostates and also reduce long-term risk of acute urinary retention. They take longer to work than alpha blockers but provide more durable improvement.

Finasteride (Proscar®) Dutasteride (Avodart®)

Often combined with an alpha blocker for men with moderate-to-large prostates. Also reduces PSA — important to know when monitoring for prostate cancer.

Treats BPH + ED

Tadalafil (Cialis®) — Two Problems, One Pill

Tadalafil — best known as Cialis for erectile dysfunction — is also FDA-approved for the treatment of BPH. It works by relaxing smooth muscle in both the prostate and bladder, improving urinary flow while simultaneously treating erectile dysfunction. For men dealing with both BPH and ED, daily low-dose tadalafil is an elegant, evidence-based solution that addresses both conditions at once.

Cialis® (Tadalafil) 5mg Daily FDA-Approved for BPH + ED

If you have both urinary symptoms and erectile dysfunction — and many men do — this is often Dr. Kashani's first recommendation. One daily pill. Two major quality-of-life improvements.

Combination

Combination Therapy

For men with moderate-to-severe BPH symptoms and a large prostate, combining an alpha blocker with a 5-alpha reductase inhibitor produces better and more durable results than either drug alone. The alpha blocker provides immediate relief while the 5-ARI gradually shrinks the prostate over months.

Dutasteride + Tamsulosin (Jalyn®) Custom Combination

Clinical trials show combination therapy significantly reduces the risk of symptom progression and the need for surgery compared to either drug alone.

Minimally Invasive & Surgical Procedures for BPH

When medications don't provide enough relief — or when the prostate is large enough that surgery is the better long-term option — several excellent procedures are available. Dr. Kashani performs TURP and works closely with colleagues who offer Aquablation and UroLift.

Dr. Kashani Performs

TURP — Transurethral Resection of the Prostate

TURP is the gold standard surgical treatment for BPH — the procedure by which all others are measured. A resectoscope is passed through the urethra and a wire loop removes excess prostate tissue, opening the urinary channel. No incisions. No general surgery. TURP has the most evidence behind it of any BPH procedure and produces the most durable long-term results.

Gold Standard Most Durable Results Hospital Procedure General or Spinal Anesthesia

Dr. Kashani performs TURP at the hospital. Most patients go home within 1 to 2 days with a temporary catheter for a few days. Long-term success rate exceeds 85%.

Aquablation

Aquablation uses a real-time ultrasound-guided robotic waterjet to precisely remove prostate tissue — without heat. This preserves the surrounding nerves and structures better than traditional resection, making it an excellent option for men who are particularly concerned about sexual side effects. It is most effective for medium to large prostates.

Robotic Waterjet Nerve-Sparing Medium–Large Prostates Hospital Procedure

Dr. Kashani will refer you to a trusted colleague who performs Aquablation when this is the best option for your situation. His network ensures you receive expert care regardless of which procedure is right for you.

Colleague Referral Available

UroLift®

UroLift is a minimally invasive office-based procedure that uses small implants to hold the enlarged prostate lobes apart — like opening a curtain — without cutting, heating, or removing any tissue. It has a very rapid recovery and preserves sexual function, including ejaculation. Best suited for men with smaller prostates and who want to avoid surgery or general anesthesia.

No Tissue Removal Preserves Ejaculation Smaller Prostates Office-Based Option

Dr. Kashani will refer you to a trusted colleague who performs UroLift when it is the most appropriate choice for your anatomy and goals.

Watchful Waiting

Active Surveillance

For men with mild symptoms that are not significantly affecting quality of life, watchful waiting with lifestyle modifications is a reasonable approach. This means regular monitoring without medication or procedures, combined with fluid management, caffeine and alcohol reduction, and bladder training techniques.

Mild Symptoms Only Lifestyle Modifications Regular Monitoring

Not appropriate for men with urinary retention, kidney damage, or moderate-to-severe symptoms. Dr. Kashani will advise honestly whether this is a safe option for you.

Overactive Bladder (OAB) — Treatment Options

From behavioral therapy and daily medication to Botox bladder injections

Medical Management for Overactive Bladder

OAB medications work by calming the overactive bladder muscle — reducing urgency, frequency, and nighttime trips to the bathroom. Several classes are available with different side effect profiles.

Beta-3 Agonist

Vibegron (Gemtesa®)

Gemtesa is one of the newest and best-tolerated OAB medications available. It works by activating beta-3 receptors in the bladder, relaxing the detrusor muscle and increasing bladder capacity. Critically, it does not cross the blood-brain barrier — meaning it has essentially no cognitive side effects, making it an excellent choice for older men. Taken once daily.

Gemtesa® (Vibegron) Once Daily No Brain Fog FDA Approved

Dr. Kashani often prefers Gemtesa for its excellent tolerability and minimal side effects compared to older anticholinergic agents.

Beta-3 Agonist

Mirabegron (Myrbetriq®)

Similar mechanism to Gemtesa — a beta-3 agonist that relaxes the bladder muscle. Well-established with a long track record. Also taken once daily. Can slightly raise blood pressure in some patients, so not ideal for men with poorly controlled hypertension.

Myrbetriq® (Mirabegron) Once Daily Well-Tolerated

A good alternative when Gemtesa is not covered by insurance, with similar efficacy and tolerability.

Anticholinergic

Anticholinergic Medications

The older class of OAB medications — still effective but with more side effects including dry mouth, constipation, and (importantly) potential cognitive effects with long-term use in older adults. Generally used when beta-3 agonists are not tolerated or covered. The extended-release formulations have better tolerability than immediate-release.

Oxybutynin (Ditropan®) Tolterodine (Detrol®) Solifenacin (Vesicare®) Trospium

Use with caution in older men. Dr. Kashani avoids immediate-release oxybutynin in elderly patients due to cognitive risk.

Behavioral

Bladder Training & Lifestyle

Behavioral strategies can significantly reduce OAB symptoms — sometimes as effectively as medication. Bladder training teaches the brain to gradually extend the time between urination. Fluid management (timing, type, and amount of fluid intake) reduces urgency. Pelvic floor exercises strengthen the muscles that help suppress urgency.

Bladder Training Fluid Management Pelvic Floor Therapy Caffeine & Alcohol Reduction

Always recommended alongside medication — the combination of behavioral therapy and medication produces better outcomes than either alone.

Bladder Botox Injections — When Medications Aren't Enough

Yes — the same Botox used cosmetically is injected directly into the bladder muscle to treat severe overactive bladder and urge incontinence. It is FDA-approved, highly effective, and a game-changer for patients who haven't responded to oral medications.

Botox works by temporarily blocking the nerve signals that cause the bladder muscle to contract involuntarily. A small amount is injected into multiple sites in the bladder wall during a brief in-office or outpatient cystoscopy procedure. Results typically last 6 to 12 months, after which the injections can be repeated.

Most patients experience dramatic improvement — significantly fewer episodes of urgency, leakage, and nighttime urination. Many describe it as life-changing after years of struggling with medications that provided only partial relief.

Important consideration: Bladder Botox can sometimes cause urinary retention — difficulty fully emptying the bladder. Dr. Kashani will assess your baseline voiding function before recommending this treatment, and will teach patients on clean intermittent catheterization (CIC) as a precaution if needed.

  • FDA-approved for OAB and urge incontinence
  • Results last 6 to 12 months
  • Quick in-office or outpatient procedure
  • Excellent option after oral medications have failed
  • Can be repeated indefinitely as needed

Frequently Asked Questions

Is BPH the same as prostate cancer?

No — BPH is completely separate from prostate cancer. BPH is a benign (non-cancerous) enlargement of the prostate. However, both conditions can cause similar urinary symptoms, and both can elevate PSA levels. This is why Dr. Kashani always ensures an appropriate PSA evaluation is performed as part of the workup for any man with urinary symptoms.

How do I know if my problem is BPH or overactive bladder?

They often overlap — many men have both. In general, BPH tends to cause more obstructive symptoms (weak stream, straining, incomplete emptying), while OAB causes more irritative symptoms (urgency, frequency, leakage). A thorough evaluation including flow rate testing and post-void residual measurement helps distinguish between the two. Dr. Kashani will clarify the diagnosis before recommending treatment.

Can tadalafil really help with both my urinary symptoms and ED at the same time?

Yes — and this is one of the most useful pharmacological options in men's health. Daily low-dose tadalafil (5mg) is FDA-approved for both BPH and erectile dysfunction. Clinical trials show meaningful improvement in both IPSS urinary symptom scores and erectile function scores with a single daily pill. For men dealing with both conditions — which is very common — it is an excellent first-line option that Dr. Kashani frequently prescribes.

How is TURP different from UroLift and Aquablation?

TURP removes prostate tissue, creating a permanent channel through the prostate. It is the most durable procedure with the longest track record. UroLift does not remove tissue — it holds the prostate lobes apart with implants, preserving ejaculation and requiring minimal recovery. Aquablation uses a waterjet to remove tissue with a nerve-sparing robotic approach. Each has advantages depending on prostate size, anatomy, patient age, and priorities. Dr. Kashani will help you understand which is right for you — and refer you to the appropriate specialist when needed.

Does TURP affect sexual function?

TURP can cause retrograde ejaculation — where semen goes backward into the bladder instead of forward during orgasm. This results in a "dry" orgasm — the sensation is intact but there is no ejaculate. It occurs in the majority of TURP patients and is considered an expected outcome, not a complication. Erectile function is generally preserved. Dr. Kashani discusses this in detail before surgery so patients can make a fully informed decision.

How long does bladder Botox last?

Results typically last 6 to 12 months. Most patients return for repeat injections once symptoms return. The procedure can be performed indefinitely with consistent results. There is no cumulative toxicity and no development of resistance — each round of injections works as well as the last.

Is it normal to get up multiple times at night to urinate?

Getting up once per night is considered within normal limits. Getting up 2 or more times — called nocturia — is a medical symptom worth addressing. Nocturia significantly disrupts sleep, increases fall risk in older men, and affects daytime energy and function. It is very common but not inevitable — and it is highly treatable with the right evaluation and management.

Will my insurance cover BPH or OAB treatment?

Medical management (office visits and prescription medications) is almost always covered. Surgical procedures like TURP are typically covered by insurance and Medicare when medically indicated. Newer medications like Gemtesa may require prior authorization. Dr. Kashani's team will verify your benefits and assist with any authorizations needed before treatment is initiated.
