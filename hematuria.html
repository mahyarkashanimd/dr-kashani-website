Blood in Urine — Causes, Types, What the Workup Involves, and When to Be Concerned

Blood in Urine: A Finding That Always Deserves Attention

Seeing blood in your urine — or being told your urine test shows red blood cells — can be alarming. The good news: most causes are benign. The critical point: every case deserves a proper evaluation.

Hematuria — the medical term for blood in the urine — is one of the most important urologic findings a physician can encounter. It can be the only early sign of bladder cancer. It can indicate kidney stones, a UTI, an enlarged prostate, or kidney disease. Without a proper workup, you simply cannot know — and not knowing is not acceptable.

Dr. Kashani performs the complete hematuria evaluation — cystoscopy, CT urogram, and urine cytology — in a systematic, efficient, and patient-focused manner. Most patients have a clear answer within one to two visits.

Do not ignore blood in your urine: Even a single episode of visible blood in the urine that resolves on its own still requires evaluation. "It went away" does not mean it was not significant.

Types of Hematuria

Hematuria is classified by whether blood is visible or only detectable on testing — and by when in the urine stream the blood appears.

Gross Hematuria

Blood visible to the naked eye — urine that appears pink, red, orange-brown, or cola-colored. Gross hematuria causes significant alarm and rightly prompts immediate evaluation. Any single episode of gross hematuria warrants a full urologic workup, regardless of whether it resolves spontaneously.

Microscopic Hematuria

Red blood cells detected on urinalysis but not visible to the eye. Defined as 3 or more red blood cells per high-power field on a properly collected specimen. Microscopic hematuria is not less important than gross hematuria — it can be the only early finding in bladder cancer or kidney disease.

Initial Hematuria

Blood appearing at the start of urination and clearing midstream. This pattern suggests a urethral or prostate source — bleeding from the urethra or prostate mixes with urine at the beginning of the stream.

Terminal Hematuria

Blood appearing at the end of urination. This pattern is most associated with bladder pathology — particularly bladder neck or trigone lesions. Terminal hematuria is a red flag pattern that always warrants cystoscopy.

Total Hematuria

Blood present throughout the entire urine stream. This pattern suggests an upper urinary tract source (kidney or ureter) or a diffuse bladder lesion. CT urogram is especially important when total hematuria is present.

Painless Hematuria

Blood in the urine without pain. This is the classic presentation of bladder cancer — and the primary reason why painless gross hematuria demands urgent evaluation. Do not be reassured by the absence of pain.

Common Causes of Hematuria

From benign to serious — understanding what might be causing blood in your urine

What Causes Blood in the Urine?

The urinary tract includes the kidneys, ureters, bladder, prostate (in men), and urethra. Bleeding can originate anywhere along this system. A thorough workup is designed to examine each component systematically.

Most Common

Kidney Stones

Stones moving through the ureter scratch and irritate the lining, causing bleeding. Often accompanied by severe flank pain (renal colic), but stones can cause microscopic hematuria silently. CT urogram detects virtually all stones in the urinary tract.

If kidney stones are identified as the cause, Dr. Kashani provides full management — from observation and medical expulsion therapy to surgical referral when needed. Learn about kidney stone treatment →

Very Common

Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)

Infection of the bladder (cystitis) causes inflammation and irritation that can lead to microscopic or gross hematuria, usually with burning urination and frequency. If hematuria clears completely after treating a UTI and a repeat urinalysis is negative, further workup may not be needed in low-risk patients.

In men, a UTI with hematuria always warrants further evaluation — UTIs in men are less common and more likely to have an underlying structural cause.

Common in Men

Enlarged Prostate (BPH)

BPH can cause hematuria through several mechanisms — enlarged surface blood vessels on the prostate can rupture, and urinary retention can irritate the bladder. In men over 50 with hematuria and urinary symptoms, BPH is often part of the picture — though it does not replace the need for a complete workup.

BPH and hematuria can coexist with other causes. A complete workup always includes cystoscopy even when BPH seems to explain the bleeding. Learn about BPH treatment →

Must Rule Out

Bladder Cancer

Painless gross hematuria is the classic — and often only — presentation of bladder cancer. It accounts for roughly 2–3% of hematuria cases but is the reason that every episode of hematuria demands cystoscopy. Bladder cancer is highly treatable when caught early. Cystoscopy allows direct visualization of the bladder lining to identify and biopsy any suspicious lesions.

The entire purpose of the hematuria evaluation is to identify this diagnosis when it is present. A negative cystoscopy and CT urogram provides meaningful reassurance.

Upper Tract

Kidney Cancer & Kidney Disease

Kidney tumors — renal cell carcinoma — can bleed into the collecting system and cause hematuria, often with no other symptoms until the tumor is quite large. CT urogram is the key test for upper tract evaluation. Glomerulonephritis (kidney inflammation) and IgA nephropathy are medical kidney diseases that also cause hematuria, typically detected by proteinuria on urinalysis and managed with nephrology.

Upper tract sources are evaluated by CT urogram. When the cause appears to be a medical (non-surgical) kidney disease, Dr. Kashani coordinates with nephrology.

Other Causes

Other Urologic Causes

Urethral stricture, bladder stones, ureteral tumors, radiation cystitis (from prior pelvic radiation), vigorous exercise ("runner's hematuria"), and blood thinners (anticoagulants) can all cause hematuria. Anticoagulant use does not eliminate the need for a hematuria workup — it is associated with finding significant underlying lesions.

Being on blood thinners does not explain away hematuria. Guidelines recommend the same workup regardless of anticoagulant use.

The Hematuria Workup

Three tests. A clear picture of the entire urinary tract.

What the Evaluation Involves

The standard hematuria workup recommended by the American Urological Association consists of three complementary tests that together evaluate the entire urinary tract from kidneys to urethra.

Test 1 — Dr. Kashani Performs

Cystoscopy

A thin, flexible camera (cystoscope) is passed through the urethra into the bladder under topical local anesthesia. The entire urethra, prostate (in men), and bladder lining are directly visualized. Any abnormal areas — lesions, tumors, inflammation, stones — are identified and can be biopsied. Dr. Kashani performs flexible cystoscopy in the office. The procedure takes approximately 5 minutes. Most patients return to normal activities the same day.

Cystoscopy is the only test that can directly visualize the bladder lining. It cannot be replaced by imaging alone for hematuria evaluation.

Test 2

CT Urogram

A CT scan with intravenous contrast specifically designed to evaluate the upper urinary tract — kidneys, ureters, and bladder. The CT urogram identifies kidney tumors, kidney stones, ureteral abnormalities, and upper tract lesions that cannot be seen on cystoscopy. It is the gold standard imaging study for hematuria evaluation and is ordered through radiology with results reviewed by Dr. Kashani.

CT urogram and cystoscopy are complementary — one sees the upper tract, the other the lower tract. Both are needed for a complete evaluation.

Test 3

Urine Cytology

A voided urine specimen is sent to pathology for microscopic analysis of the cells shed from the urinary tract lining. Cytology is most useful for detecting high-grade bladder cancer and carcinoma in situ — a flat, non-papillary type of bladder cancer that can be difficult to see on cystoscopy. It is less sensitive for low-grade tumors but adds an important layer of detection.

Urine cytology is a simple, non-invasive test that completes the hematuria evaluation. A positive cytology always requires further investigation even with a normal cystoscopy.

Additional

Urinalysis & Urine Culture

A complete urinalysis is always the starting point — it confirms the presence of red blood cells, identifies white blood cells (suggesting infection), detects protein (suggesting kidney disease), and screens for other abnormalities. A urine culture is ordered when infection is suspected. These initial tests help direct the subsequent workup and rule out simple explanations before proceeding to cystoscopy and CT.

Never start a hematuria workup without a complete urinalysis with microscopy on a properly collected specimen. This step can change the entire direction of evaluation.

Frequently Asked Questions — Hematuria

Is blood in urine always serious?

Blood in urine always warrants urologic evaluation — even a single episode, even if it resolves on its own, and even if you have no other symptoms. The most common causes are benign (UTI, kidney stones, BPH), but bladder cancer and kidney cancer can present with painless blood in the urine as their only early sign. The evaluation is straightforward and the peace of mind is worth it.

What is the difference between gross and microscopic hematuria?

Gross hematuria means blood is visible to the naked eye — urine that is pink, red, or brown. Microscopic hematuria means red blood cells are found on a urine test (urinalysis) but the urine looks normal. Both types require urologic evaluation. Gross hematuria causes more alarm, but microscopic hematuria is not less important — it can be the only sign of a significant underlying condition.

What does a hematuria workup involve?

The standard evaluation includes three components: cystoscopy (direct visualization of the bladder), CT urogram (imaging of the kidneys, ureters, and bladder), and urine cytology (microscopic analysis of urine cells). Dr. Kashani performs cystoscopy in the office under local anesthesia — it takes about 5 minutes and most patients go back to normal activities the same day.

Can a UTI cause blood in urine?

Yes — urinary tract infections are a common cause of blood in the urine. If hematuria resolves completely after treating a UTI and a repeat urinalysis is clear, further workup may not be needed in low-risk patients. However, in men and in women with risk factors (smoking history, prior pelvic radiation, chemical exposures), a complete workup is appropriate regardless of whether a UTI was identified.

Is cystoscopy painful?

Cystoscopy is performed under topical local anesthesia in the office. Most patients describe it as uncomfortable rather than painful — similar to pressure or the urge to urinate. The flexible cystoscope is thin and well-tolerated. The procedure takes about 5 minutes. There may be mild burning with urination for a day or two afterward, which resolves on its own.

Can kidney stones cause blood in the urine?

Yes — kidney stones are a very common cause of hematuria, both gross and microscopic. As a stone moves through the ureter, it can irritate and scratch the lining, causing bleeding. This is often accompanied by flank pain (renal colic), though some stones cause hematuria without pain. CT urogram — part of the standard hematuria workup — is excellent at detecting kidney stones and their location.
